Datasets & preprocessing

Our factuality models are trained on an extended version of the LIAR-PLUS dataset, augmented with more recent scraped PolitiFact data. This section documents what we used, how we processed it, and what assumptions and limitations come with those choices.

Core dataset: LIAR-PLUS

We use the LIAR-PLUS dataset (Alhindi et al. 2018), an extension of the LIAR corpus (Wang 2017), as the primary supervised signal for our factuality models. LIAR-PLUS contains over 12,800 short political statements that have been fact-checked by PolitiFact.

  • Labels: six categorical truth ratings from pants-on-fire to true.
  • Metadata: speaker name, occupation, political party, state, subject, and justification text.
  • Splits: predefined train / validation / test splits provided by the dataset authors.

These metadata fields allow us to model factuality using both linguistic features (e.g., wording, sentiment, repetition) and socio-political context (e.g., speaker, party, topic).

Augmented with recent PolitiFact scraping

LIAR-PLUS was collected several years ago. To reduce temporal bias and better match the current misinformation landscape, we merged in newer PolitiFact statements that we scraped separately.

These additional statements:

  • Follow the same short-claim format as LIAR-PLUS.
  • Reuse PolitiFact’s truth ratings and justification text where available.
  • Expand coverage to more recent political topics, actors, and narratives, making our models less anchored to a single election cycle.

All scraping respects PolitiFact’s public pages and is used solely for research and educational purposes within the context of this capstone project.

Preprocessing & feature-ready corpus

Before training, we perform several preprocessing steps to create a clean, uniform corpus across original and scraped data:

  • Standardize column names across splits and sources.
  • Remove redundant index or ID columns that do not carry information.
  • Normalize text: strip whitespace, convert to lowercase during tokenization, and remove file suffixes such as “.json” from IDs.
  • Convert metadata fields into consistent formats (e.g., categorical encodings for party, binary indicators for subject domains).
  • Derive helper variables for later feature extraction (counts, ratios, flags).

The result is a dataset that can be fed directly into our feature engineering pipelines for the four factuality factors, with reproducible transformations implemented in code.

Factuality factors derived from data

From this corpus, we derive labels and features tailored to each of our four factuality factors:

Frequency heuristic
Captures how strongly a statement implies truth by repetition, buzzwords, or appeals to common belief. Features include: TF-IDF mean, average token frequency across the corpus, a buzzword score based on generalized phrases (“always”, “everyone”, “experts agree”), and a repetition ratio.
Sensationalism
Measures emotional exaggeration and dramatic framing. We engineer features like exclamation counts, all-caps tokens, and frequencies of sensational terms (“shocking”, “explosive”, “unprecedented”), plus sentiment polarity and subjectivity scores. These are combined with TF-IDF vectors.
Malicious account
Approximates language associated with inauthentic or spam-like behavior, using features such as average token length, repetition score, link count, mention count, punctuation ratio, and uppercase ratio alongside TF-IDF mean.
Naive realism
Estimates how dogmatically a statement presents its viewpoint as self-evident truth. Features include ratios of absolutist terms (“always”, “never”, “clearly”), cautious terms (“maybe”, “might”, “possibly”), and dismissive language (“biased”, “brainwashed”), plus sentiment subjectivity and polarity.

Limitations & validity considerations

While LIAR-PLUS is a widely used benchmark, it comes with important limitations:

  • The dataset focuses on short political statements, which may not capture the full complexity of long-form articles or multimodal content.
  • Labels are derived from PolitiFact’s editorial judgments, which encode specific norms and may not fully generalize across cultures or domains.
  • Our additional scraping introduces potential selection bias toward higher profile, fact-checked claims.

To mitigate leakage and validity issues, we:

  • Respect the original train / validation / test splits for supervised models.
  • Keep scraped data disjoint from our hand-labeled article evaluation set used for reporting accuracy.
  • Avoid directly training on the specific news articles later used to evaluate the multi-agent system.

For full reproducibility details, including exact preprocessing scripts and feature definitions, please see the project repository on GitHub.
