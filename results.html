Results

Key results & evaluation

Our main result is that carefully structured prompting—particularly In-Context Learning and Fractal Chain-of-Thought—substantially improves factuality factor accuracy compared to naive baselines, while reducing hallucinations and misclassifications.

We evaluate all prompting strategies on 40 hand-labeled news articles and report accuracy per factuality factor.

Factor-wise findings

Frequency heuristic
Accuracy jumps from 0.40 (CoT) to 0.675 with ICL and stabilizes around 0.65 with FCoT 4. The big gain from ICL suggests that clearly labeled examples are especially effective for teaching the model the difference between legitimate consensus and manipulative “everyone knows” rhetoric.
Malicious account
The Malicious Account factor sees the most dramatic improvement, from 0.10 (base) to 0.95 (FCoT 3–4). Early prompts often over-predicted deception, even hallucinating article sources. FCoT’s hallucination constraints and multi-iteration analysis make the agent much more conservative and accurate.
Sensationalism
Sensationalism accuracy is relatively stable across prompts, hovering around 0.50–0.68. FCoT 4 gives the best performance (0.675) but the gain over ICL is modest, suggesting that simple lexical and sentiment features already capture much of the signal.
Naive realism
Naive Realism is the hardest factor to optimize. Accuracy improves only slightly from 0.38 (base) to the mid-0.40s for most advanced prompts and remains the lowest-performing factor overall. This suggests that dogmatic perspective-taking is more subtle and context-dependent than the other heuristics.

Qualitative evaluation & failure modes

Our case study on the Daily Wire article highlights how prompts affect qualitative behavior:

  • Base prompts sometimes treat biased but legitimate outlets as “clearly deceptive”, hallucinating links to satire sites or mislabeling political roles.
  • Early prompts conflate quoted rhetoric with author voice, penalizing articles that are simply reporting extreme statements rather than endorsing them.
  • FCoT prompts, especially FCoT 4, are more likely to attribute claims correctly to identifiable public figures and to differentiate reporting from endorsement.

Remaining failure modes include:

  • Difficulty distinguishing satire or parody content without explicit cues in the text.
  • Limited robustness on topics or linguistic styles very different from the political content in LIAR-PLUS.
  • Occasional overconfidence when the article is short and ambiguous.

Computation cost vs. reliability

Fractal Chain-of-Thought improves accuracy and reduces hallucination, but it does so by:

  • Enforcing three internal analysis iterations per agent.
  • Requiring explicit coverage checklists and objective scores per iteration.
  • Running four factuality agents in parallel for each article, each with its own tool calls.

This leads to noticeably higher token usage and wall-clock time compared to base prompts. For research and high-stakes analysis, this trade-off is acceptable; for real-time deployment, one might prefer a lighter prompt (e.g., ICL + tool calling) that balances cost and performance.

Implications & next steps

Our results support three broader claims:

  • Reasoning scaffolds matter at least as much as model size. Well-designed prompts and multi-step reasoning can substantially improve factuality evaluation without changing the underlying base model.
  • Confidence calibration and hallucination constraints are essential for trustworthy LLM-based evaluators.
  • Refining factor definitions (especially for Malicious Account and Naive Realism) can align model outputs much more closely with human judgments.

Future work should:

  • Scale evaluation to larger and more diverse article sets.
  • Test cross-model generalization beyond the specific Gemini variants used here.
  • Explore automated ways to design objective functions tailored to new misinformation heuristics.
